WHERE ARE THE PARKS?

Tribal acknowledgements

We would like to acknowledge that the land on which Port parks are located are within the traditional territories of the Coast Salish peoples, keepers and protectors of both land and waterways. The Port is also engaging local tribes around shaping access to and providing amenities at Port parks.

In 1971, Seattle’s Shoreline Management Act passed which protected and mandated any new development along shorelines, including the Duwamish River, maintain areas for the public to access the waterfront.

In 1985, to comply with the Shoreline Management Act, the Port of Seattle created public access points along the Lower Duwamish Waterway as mitigation sites to compensate local communities for industrial development on Port properties along the waterway. The Port’s goals were:

  • To provide “a wide range of use opportunities” on the waterfront
  • To make public improvements to waterway access
  • To encourage public use of the waterway
  • To increase land devoted to public access in the Lower Duwamish Waterway

These public access points, now known as parks, were designed to give people different ways to access the Duwamish River, whether fishing, boating, or sharing a meal. In 2007, the Port approved the Seaport Shoreline Access Plan which included “long term planned/potential uses” for the parks, including:

  • Preserve, maintain and expand habitat mitigation
  • Enhance and expand public access

Access to the Port’s shoreline parks as well as current amenities were studied in 2018 by a graduate student intern and in 2020 by the Port of Seattle Community Action Team (PCAT) youth interns.
